Rewiring Connections

I sometimes reflect on my teenage experiences, and I consistently find myself fixating on events that were particularly traumatic for me. Many people who experience trauma often display increased neural activity in most regions of the brain, but with deliberate effort, it is possible to reorganize neural pathways and reduce overactivity in certain areas. So, I am dedicating the next 100 days to exploring whether I can rewire my brain's associations with my teenage years.

Every day, the intent is to sketch a new memory from my teenage years.

Trigger warning: some of the illustrations may contain content of sexual assault, abuse, suicide attempt and violence.

Grateful for Amisha Kumar, Jatin Yashroy, Aananya Parihar, Aaranyah Pandey, and my family for letting me tell my story in the truest version I remember.

Amitriptyline-marked nodes have denser neurite branching and more opaque axon connections, reflecting heightened synaptic activity during medication.

If I intentionally look for a memory, I will categorize it as a low activity memory. If a memory surfaces unexpectedly, I'll label it a high activity memory.

If involuntary memories surface while looking for one, I'll sketch the voluntary one first and the involuntary ones in the next few days.

I'll mark physical sensations: a wave symbol for weak/low-intensity feelings, a zigzag for strong/high-intensity. Neurons with strong emotions are more opaque.

I'll also mark first- or third-person perspective. People with PTSD may view memories from a third-person perspective to create emotional distance.
